# Web writing best practices

A sourced checklist of current tactics for writing long-form web content — blog posts, articles,
guides. Use it as a pre-flight check, not a recipe — the user's own voice and the skill's core
rules win over any single tactic.

## Write for scanning, not reading

- [ ] Assume the reader scans. Eye-tracking work found 79% of people scan a new page and only 16%
      read word-for-word; on an average page people read at most ~28% of the words. Structure for
      the scanner first.
- [ ] Front-load everything. Readers sweep the page in an F-shape — first line, a shorter second
      sweep, then down the left edge — so the right side of lower lines is often skipped. Put the
      key point first in the post, in each section, in each paragraph, and in each list item.
- [ ] Lead with the conclusion (inverted pyramid). Don't save the payoff for a reveal; the reader
      may never reach it. Roughly half of visitors leave in under 15 seconds.
- [ ] Make it half the length you'd write for print. Concise text, scannable layout, and plain
      (non-promotional) language together measured a 124% gain in usability in Nielsen's study.

## The hook / intro

- [ ] Open on the reader's problem in their own words, then promise the payoff and preview how the
      post delivers it (the Agree–Promise–Preview shape). Keep the hook to a sentence or three.
- [ ] Lead with a concrete pull: a surprising stat, a bold or contrarian claim, a one-line story,
      or the tension the reader already feels. No "in today's…" throat-clearing.
- [ ] A hook promises only what the post delivers. The intro is where the reader decides if you
      understand their search; earn the scroll, don't bait it.

## Structure & scannability

- [ ] Descriptive subheads that work as a map. A reader skimming only the H2s should get the gist.
      Phrase some as the question the reader is searching — it also helps the post surface in AI
      summaries.
- [ ] Add a subheading before any section runs past ~300 words; no long, unbroken wall of text.
- [ ] Short paragraphs: one idea each, 2–4 sentences, under ~150 words. White space is a feature.
- [ ] Use bulleted and numbered lists for steps, options, or anything parallel — they reset the
      eye and pull people in better than prose.
- [ ] One H1 (the title). Nest H2s for major sections and H3s for sub-points; don't skip levels.
- [ ] Bold the occasional load-bearing phrase to break the leftward scan — sparingly, or it stops
      working.

## Readability

- [ ] Keep most sentences under ~20 words; flag yourself if more than ~25% run longer. Vary
      length so it reads with rhythm, not as a metronome.
- [ ] Active voice by default. Hold passive constructions under ~10% of sentences — passive reads
      distant and wordy.
- [ ] Plain words over jargon. Read it aloud; if a phrase wouldn't survive a conversation with a
      colleague, rewrite it.

## Length & depth

- [ ] Match length to intent, not a word target — Google is explicit there's no magic count. A
      quick answer wants 300–500 words; a how-to or standard post often lands ~1,500–2,500; a deep
      or competitive guide can run further when the subject earns it.
- [ ] Benchmark against what already ranks: read the top few results and cover the topic at least
      as completely, then stop. A tight post that fully answers the question beats a padded one.
- [ ] For a transactional or navigational query, stay short and get to the point — long-form is
      the wrong tool there.

## SEO basics

- [ ] One unique, descriptive title tag and meta description per post — write the title with the
      main keyword near the front and the description for the click. Exact character limits are a
      technical SEO concern outside this rubric.
- [ ] Short, lowercase, hyphen-separated slug with the keyword; drop dates and stop words
      (`/test-the-seam`, not `/2026/06/how-to-test-the-seam-in-your-app`).
- [ ] 3–5 contextual internal links to relevant posts, placed early where they carry weight. Use
      descriptive anchor text ("our guide to integration tests"), never "click here", and vary it.
- [ ] Descriptive alt text on meaningful images, ~125 characters or fewer; skip "image of", end
      with a period, and use a real keyword naturally in at least one. Leave purely decorative
      images with empty `alt=""`.

## Close

- [ ] End on a single clear takeaway, then one focused call to action that follows from the post —
      not a pile of competing asks. Two to five words of action ("Try it on one flaky test").
- [ ] No "in conclusion", no summary that just restates the post. Leave the reader with the one
      thing to remember or do.
